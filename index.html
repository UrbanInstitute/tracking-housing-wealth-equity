Tracking Homeownership Wealth Gaps

Is Housing Wealth Equitable in Your City?

October XX, 2021

Homeownership is a vital tool for building wealth and economic security, but households of color are less likely to own homes than white households. And on average, Black, Hispanic and Other non-white homeowners excluding Asians typically own homes of lower value than white homeowners.

Partly due to the long history of segregation, redlining, displacement, and other discriminatory practices accumulating wealth through homeownership is harder for many families of color. And the data bear that out.

In 2019, the value of all primary residences in the US was an estimated $23.6 trillion. White households held a disproportionate amount of that wealth, making up two-thirds of the homeowners but holding three-quarters of the primary residence housing wealth.

This data tool and accompanying report explore cities’ aggregate housing wealth to uncover the drivers of racial and ethnic housing gaps. Search for your city below to see how housing wealth is distributed where you live.

What drives housing inequity in ?

Homeownership rates and home values play a role in determining a group’s aggregate housing wealth. Nationwide, Black, Hispanic, Asian and other households of color, hold a smaller share of housing wealth because they are less likely to own a primary residence. And on average, Black, Hispanic and Other homeowners own homes of lower value.

ABOUT

The measures calculated in this data tool come from the US Census Bureau’s 2019 American Community Survey. We chose to focus on cities rather than metropolitan areas because households of color are more likely to account for the majority of the population at the city level. Examining data at the city level can produce contrasting results between the racial and ethnic composition of a city’s household population and its primary residence housing wealth owners. Local decisions on housing policy can happen at the city happen more often at the city level than at the regional level. We include only incorporated cities in our analysis and exclude census-designated places because they lack a municipal government that can adopt policies that support economic growth or inclusion.

This data tool uses the term Hispanic to refer to people of Latino or Hispanic origin because it is consistent with the US Census Bureau’s data collection. The authors acknowledge this may not be the preferred identifier, and we remain committed to employing inclusive language whenever possible.
