Tracking Homeownership Wealth Gaps

Is Housing Wealth Equitable in Your City?

November 11, 2021

Homeownership is a vital tool for building wealth and economic security, but households of color are less likely to own homes than white households. And on average, Black, Hispanic, and other nonwhite homeowners (excluding Asian households) typically own homes of lower value than white homeowners.

Partly because of the long history of segregation, redlining, displacement, and other discriminatory practices, accumulating wealth through homeownership is harder for many families of color. And the data bear that out.

In 2019, the value of all primary residences in the US was an estimated $23.6 trillion. White households held a disproportionate amount of that wealth, making up two-thirds of the homeowners but holding three-quarters of the primary-residence housing wealth.

This data tool and accompanying report explore cities’ aggregate housing wealth to uncover the drivers of racial and ethnic housing gaps. Search for your city below to see how housing wealth is distributed where you live.

What drives housing wealth inequity in ?

Homeownership rates and home values play a role in determining a group’s aggregate housing wealth. Nationwide, Black, Hispanic, Asian, and other households of color hold a smaller share of housing wealth because they are less likely to own a primary residence. And on average, Black, Hispanic, and other homeowners own homes of lower value.

The measures calculated in this data tool come from the US Census Bureau’s 2019 American Community Survey. We use “housing wealth” to describe the total value of primary residences, not housing equity. This terminology is consistent with its use in previous research (PDF). Whereas housing equity is the difference between a home’s value and debt collateralized by that home (e.g., the mortgage or a home equity line of credit), housing wealth is the product of the average owner-occupied home value and the total number of homeowners. The number of homeowners is equal to the total number of households multiplied by the homeownership rate. Subsequently, the steps from total households to total primary-residence housing wealth include both the homeownership rate and average home values.

This data tool uses the term Hispanic to refer to people of Latino or Hispanic origin because it is consistent with the US Census Bureau’s data collection. The authors acknowledge this may not be the preferred identifier, and we remain committed to employing inclusive language whenever possible.
